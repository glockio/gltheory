Dependency Graphs — The One CS Concept You Need

Here is the only piece of computer science this theory requires. Everything that follows — gravity, time dilation, quantum superposition, entanglement — falls out of it.

When a program computes something, it rarely works in isolation. Most values depend on other values. To compute B, you first need A. To compute C, you need both B and D. This network of dependencies is called a dependency graph: nodes are values, edges are "this needs that."

The rule that makes everything interesting is this: you cannot resolve a node without first resolving every node it points to. There is no shortcut. There is no skipping. If node A has pointers to ten other nodes, you walk all ten before A can produce its value. If any of those ten have their own pointers, you walk those too. Resolution is a walk through the graph — and the cost of that walk is the cost to every node touched along the way.

Two Evaluation Modes — This Distinction Matters The dependency graph uses both lazy and strict evaluation, and knowing which is which explains everything.

Lazy (default): Most nodes are only resolved when something needs their output. A quiet particle sitting in empty space isn't computed every tick — it's cached from last time and carried forward. Its value is memoized. The graph knows it's there but defers the walk until someone depends on it. This is superposition: a pending node whose computation is deferred.

Strict (mass): Mass nodes cannot be lazy. Their cycle means every tick they must walk — the cycle closes back on itself and cannot be deferred without losing the stable configuration. Mass forces a strict traversal every single ordering step, and that traversal reaches into neighboring nodes whether they want it or not.

This is the only distinction you need. Lazy nodes cache. Strict cyclic nodes walk. The walk fans out. Neighbors pay. That's gravity.

Resolving a simple node is cheap — a small, bounded walk. Resolving mass is expensive — its self-referential cycle means the walk never truly bottoms out, and its pointers reach into neighboring nodes. Those neighbors pay the cost of being in the walk. The Rule That Produces Gravity You cannot resolve a node without walking all its pointers — including pointers that reach into nodes you think of as "separate." Mass has those pointers. Space is not separate from mass at the data layer; neighboring regions are connected to mass through the dependency graph. So resolving mass traverses those regions. That traversal consumes budget from every node it touches. The regions don't have a choice. They are in the graph.

GR is extraordinarily precise — it has predicted gravitational waves, light-bending, GPS drift, and black holes without a single failed test. Now the payoff. We don't need to invoke curved spacetime. We don't need a force carrier. We don't need sixteen coupled differential equations. We need exactly one thing: the mass subgraph's resolution walk consumes budget from every node it touches, and it fans out with distance.

Gravity is not a force. It is not curvature. It is the budget deficit created by a cyclic subgraph's resolution walk fanning out through the dependency graph every tick. Nodes near mass are deeply traversed — they accumulate fewer state changes per tick. We experience that deficit as time running slower near mass. Objects route toward the minimum-budget path through the graph. We experience that routing as gravitational attraction. No force is transmitted. No fabric bends. One subgraph is expensive, and everything in the graph pays a share of the cost.

This is what "emergent" means precisely. Gravity was never installed as a rule. The scheduler resolves nodes in dependency order, and the walk cost falls off with graph distance. That's the entire mechanism.

Time — The Revision Counter (and Why the Arrow Only Points One Way)

Time dilation — confirmed by every GPS satellite in orbit — is the fact that clocks run at different rates depending on gravity and velocity. A clock on a mountain runs slightly faster than one at sea level. A clock at a black hole's edge appears to stop entirely to a distant observer. Einstein's explanation: mass and velocity curve spacetime, and curved spacetime stretches time intervals. This is accurate. But it doesn't explain the mechanism.

GPS satellites need to be corrected by 45 microseconds every day. Clocks on mountains tick differently than clocks at sea level. At a black hole's edge, a clock appears to stop entirely. This is one of the best-tested results in physics.

The standard explanation is: mass curves spacetime, curved spacetime stretches time intervals. This is accurate as a description of what we observe. But it doesn't explain the mechanism. Here is the mechanism.

There is no time to slow down. What changes near mass is not the rate of time — it is the revision rate of a local region. A clock near a massive object accumulates fewer state changes per global ordering step. It doesn't experience slow time. Fewer things happen to it. That is all.

Time dilation is not a dimension stretching. It is a revision budget deficit. Mass consumes the resolution budget of neighboring regions each tick. Those regions complete fewer state changes. A clock in that region ticks fewer times — not because time slowed, but because fewer computations ran. The measurement is right. The metaphysics was wrong.

The Two Quantities C — The global ordering step. Not a rate in time. C is the sequencing operator. Each step is causally downstream of the previous. C never varies. It is not a clock. It is the causal index.

B — The local resolution budget. Per region. Per ordering step. Finite. This is what the massive object's dependency chain consumes from neighboring regions. Less B means fewer state changes completed per ordering step. Less B means a lower revision rate.

The Twin "Paradox" — Reframed, With a Mechanism

The traveling twin returns younger. The standard explanation: time moved slower for them during travel. The honest explanation in this framework: fewer net state changes accumulated. But this raises a question the framework must answer: why does moving fast consume revision budget? Mass consumes budget because its cyclic subgraph fans into neighbors every tick — that mechanism is clear. What's the mechanism for velocity?

Position in render-layer space is not separate from the dependency graph. Your position corresponds to your adjacency relationships in the data-layer graph — which nodes are your neighbors, which edges connect you to them. Moving means changing which nodes you're adjacent to. Changing adjacency means rewriting dependency edges. Rewriting edges is itself a computation that consumes resolution budget.

Motion is interaction with graph topology. You don't need to collide with a particle to consume budget. Changing your position in the graph costs resolution because the graph must rewrite your edge set every tick. Fast motion = high edge-update rate = high budget consumption = fewer net state changes per ordering step = slower subjective time.

What This Framework Has Not Yet Solved

Any framework that claims to explain the universe should be upfront about what it can and can't do. Here is what this one hasn't solved yet — and why those gaps matter.

Open Gap 1 — Full General Relativity Not Yet Derived The framework naturally produces Newton's law of gravity (1/r² force) and explains time dilation, black holes, and geodesics. But Einstein's full field equations — which also predict gravitational waves, the bending of starlight, Mercury's orbital precession, and frame dragging — have not been formally derived from the dependency graph topology. Until that derivation is complete, the framework must be honest: it is consistent with GR but has not reproduced it from first principles.
Open Gap 2 — The Preferred Causal Ordering Problem The global ordering step Ω is a logical necessity of the framework — a function S(Ω+1) = F(S(Ω)) requires a sequence. This implies a preferred causal ordering at the data layer. This is now stated as a confident position in Section 07: Lorentz symmetry is an emergent property of the render layer projection, not the data layer. The claim is coherent and consistent with de Broglie-Bohm interpretations. But whether the render layer projection actually produces exact Lorentz symmetry from a discrete causal ordering is not formally proven. The concrete prediction: at Planck-scale energies, tiny deviations from Lorentz symmetry should be detectable. Current experiments constrain but do not rule them out.
Open Gap 3 — E = mc² Is Intuition, Not Derivation The geometric argument for why c appears squared is compelling and consistent with the framework. It is not a formal derivation. A full derivation would require showing that the render layer produces Minkowski spacetime from dependency graph topology — something not yet done.
Open Gap 4 — Why Mass Forms At All The framework describes what mass is once it exists: a self-referential state configuration that perpetually regenerates itself. It does not explain how such a configuration arises from a non-self-referential initial state. The transition from the Big Bang — maximum energy density, presumably no stable self-referential loops — to stable protons and electrons is not accounted for. This is not a small gap.
Open Gap 5 — The Data Layer Is Inaccessible by Definition Like all interpretations of quantum mechanics, this framework proposes a structure for reality that cannot be directly measured — because we live in the render layer. The data layer's existence is inferred, not observed. Testable predictions must come from render-layer observations that differ from standard QM+GR. Three candidates: (1) Planck-scale Lorentz deviation detectable in gamma-ray burst timing, (2) decoherence timescales matching the threshold model, (3) a minimum mass unit at the Planck scale.
Open Gap 6 — The Born Rule: Direction Known, Derivation Pending The framework now has a candidate mechanism: probability equals the squared intensity of F's traversal contributions converging on a node. Complex amplitudes accumulate along paths through the below-θ subgraph, combine at convergence nodes, and the squared magnitude of the combined amplitude corresponds to the probability of a committed-state outcome at that node. This is consistent with Born's rule and provides a mechanistic interpretation. The determinism claim also resolves the "why one outcome" part — because F always produces one outcome; the distribution describes our epistemic ignorance of the full state S(Ω), not genuine indeterminism. What remains: a formal derivation showing that this accumulation-and-squaring mechanism follows necessarily from the graph traversal model's axioms, rather than being assumed. Direction clear. Derivation pending.
Why Honest Gaps Matter A framework that explains everything and has no open problems is not a framework — it is a story. The gaps above are real, they are hard, and they are the precise places where the framework needs to do more work to become a testable scientific theory rather than a compelling interpretive lens. Both things have value. This is both.
