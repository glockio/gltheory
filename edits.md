## Edit 1: Line 285 — Double slit description

**CURRENT:**
> We fire a bunch of photons one at a time between two slits at a screen. Each photon lands on the screen. Cool. But a pattern on the screen starts to emerge. Where the photons land produces something called an interference pattern — it happens when you have two travelling waves meet. But there is nothing in the room, there is only the photon. They are forced to conclude that before the photon reaches the screen, it is travelling not as a photon but a wave, and its wave goes through both slits and interferes with itself. **REPLACE WITH:**
> We fire a bunch of photons one at a time through two slits at a screen. Each photon lands on the screen as a single dot. Cool. But a pattern starts to emerge. Where the dots land produces something called an interference pattern — the kind of pattern you get when two waves overlap. But there is nothing in the room, there is only the one photon. They are forced to conclude that before the photon reaches the screen, it somehow went through both slits and interfered with itself. Now here's the kicker — when they put a detector at the slits to figure out which one it went through, the interference pattern vanishes. It only shows up if nothing at the slits forces a result.

## Edit 3: Lines 293–295 — The GL explanation of double slit

**CURRENT:**
> How do we explain this from an Event Graph? Well everything that is happening between now and the screen is an event. If it happened in reality it is an event. But those events collect and sum and we only produce the output once we are asked to produce the next layer of reality. The act of "measuring" is someone asking — it's when the events that are pending now have a consumer that requires them in its dependency tree to produce its own next layer of reality. So physicists say observer, but it's really a consumer of these events.
>
> Until there is a consumer, and when we don't "look", there is no consumer, these events are collecting. The result is collecting until you look, which is why looking after it already made its choice does not matter. Looking after it goes through the door is in the event cone.

**REPLACE WITH:**
> How do we explain this from an Event Graph? Between the source and the screen, the photon's journey is a bunch of pending events in the graph. Uncommitted. Accumulating. With nothing at the slits to force a result, those events just keep collecting — and the pending structure threads through both slits. Not metaphorically. The graph literally has paths through both openings, all still in-flight.
>
> The screen is a consumer. It's where enough events converge — enough causal density — to force a commit. And when it commits, the result comes from the shape of what's feeding into it: two paths, their operations combining. Depending on path lengths, they reinforce or cancel. That's your interference pattern.
>
> Now stick a detector at one of the slits. That detector is also a consumer. Its internals, its recording, all of it creates causal density right there at the slit. That density forces the pending events to commit before they ever reach the screen. Once committed — one path. One path means nothing to interfere with. Pattern gone.
>
> The detector doesn't bump the photon. It doesn't disturb it. It just creates enough going on at that spot to force a result early. Even a detector you never bother to read kills the pattern, because this has nothing to do with "looking." Physicists say observer. It's really just a consumer — something downstream that needs those events to build its own next layer of reality.
>
> And this is why the delayed choice version doesn't matter either. Measuring after the photon already went through the slit? That measurement was always going to be there. It's in the event cone. The causal density was always going to force that commit. The result was always going to be what it was.

## Edit 5: Lines 305–309 — The interpretation

**CURRENT:**
> This is probably the strongest argument I can make to show that these events are pending. Collecting the information then losing it is a null operation if there never actually is a consumer. It is a +1, -1.
>
> If it was truly random, what you do to the entangled twin should not influence the latter. Random is random, but these are correlated — you know what to look for.
>
> The dots on the screen are committed and never move. But each photon has a twin, and they share a common origin in the graph — a common causal ancestor. Their outcomes were always correlated. How the twin commits determines which subset of dots reveals a pattern. The pattern was always there, hidden in the correlations. The twin's measurement is the sorting key. Nothing was erased. Nothing went backwards. The graph knew from the start.

**REPLACE WITH:**
> This is probably the strongest argument I can make for this model. If it was truly random, what you do to the entangled twin should not influence anything. Random is random. But these are correlated — and the correlations were baked in at the crystal, when the pair was born from the same causal event.
>
> The dots on the screen never move. Nothing changes retroactively. The eraser doesn't undo a measurement — it changes what the measurement can tell you about which slit. And when it can't tell you which slit, the signals paired with those twins still carry the two-path structure from their shared origin. The pattern was always in the dots, hiding in the correlations. The twin's measurement is the sorting key that lets you see it.
