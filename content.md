---

From the rubber duck's perspective, reality — everything it experiences — is the surface of the water. But its experience is actually composed of the billions of H₂O molecules it floats on.

Our universe is the same, you exist on a massive ocean of events that all lead you to your "here" and "now". Trace any moment of your life and what you will find is there is always a sequence of events that brought you to where you are now. This idea of "here" and "now", as another name, "space" and "time", or Spacetime.

You are floating on an ocean of past events that all brought you to your "here", and "now".

So what is this "ocean of events"? We are going to refer to them as the **Event Graph.**

The Event Graph, is a series of interconnected events that when summed together give you the next "now" in reality for any point in time. These events go all the way back to the big bang.

## Chapter 3: The Event Graph

---

So it's not water you are floating on, but a graph.

More specifically, it's all the events that have ever happened, going all the way back to the big bang.

These events connect to each other, add together, and bring you to your "here" and "now." The event graph is the bookkeeping ledger of the universe, always growing.

The idea that the universe can be represented as a graph of discrete events is not as odd as it sounds. It is a real branch of physics, and this framework draws heavily from [causal set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_sets), [Wolfram's project](https://www.wolframphysics.org/), and [loop quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity).

Intuitively, what we are describing is basic cause and effect. Trace any moment in your life backwards and you'll find a chain of events that got you there. Your "now" didn't appear from nothing. It was built.

So what does this graph actually look like? A graph is just dots connected by lines. You already know graphs — a family tree is a graph, a recipe is a graph, a road map is a graph.

Ours has four rules:

**1. The dots are events.** Not particles. Not objects. Not "Sara went for a run" — there is no Sara in the graph. The dots are events, events are the bookkeeping currency of the universe. What is an Event exactly? To be honest I don't know. It would have to be the smallest unit of change that can occur. For the purpose of this article, I'd suggest it's energy in some form — which would explain why energy can't be created or destroyed. You're always just logging interactions.

**2. Nodes are stateless.** This one is a bit counterintuitive, so stay with me. The nodes themselves hold no values. All properties emerge from their position in the graph. Just like in a family tree — you don't write "parent" on a dot. You look at who's underneath it. A node in the event graph works the same way. What it _is_ comes entirely from the paths reaching it and the operations those paths carried. Nodes store nothing. They inherit everything. This matters a lot later.

**3. The lines are operations.** They carry the math. Each line connects one event to another and says "here's what happened between these two moments." Sum all the operations pointing into any dot and you get the next event. That's how the graph builds the next layer of reality.

**4. The lines point forward.** Cause to effect. Past to future. The graph only grows. This is time — not a clock on a wall, but the graph getting one layer taller.

That's the whole machine. Events, operations between them, growing forward. New events are born from the sum of what came before. Your "now" is the newest layer. The graph underneath is every "now" that already happened.

Now, there is one other very important property of this graph. Our universe has a speed limit — the speed of light, C — and so does our graph.

The speed of light has nothing to do with light. It is the maximum rate that change can occur in our universe. The fastest information can travel, and compute, within the graph. So C, is the fastest our graph can "grow".

Some regions of the graph have more going on than others. A star has more events in play than an empty patch of space. A black hole has more going on than a star. Our graph has dense regions and sparse regions.

If the graph has a fixed speed limit for how fast it can process — and dense regions have more to process — then dense regions take longer to get to their next "now" than sparse ones.

This means our graph is **not** growing evenly. And that unevenness is where things get interesting.

## Chapter 5: Mass

To answer the question of mass I want to first talk a bit about time.

Time, is an interesting concept in physics, because our equations work equally well both forwards and backwards in time.

Imagine what it would look like to you if "time", stopped. Well, nothing would change. Our perception or what we call **time is really a measure of change in our local region**.

We have already said that the speed of light C, is really the fastest information flows though our graph.

We also said that our graph is composed of events, and that the idea of objects or photons do not exist at the graph level. So what is mass then?

Mass, objects, particles are all the result of stable configurations within the graph. A collection of events that join together and form a specific "shape" or result if you will, and whose topology repeats into the "next now".

When we go to build the next layer of reality, to move from current now to future now, we sum all of those events. And for mass — when you sum all the operations together — it just generates the same shape again. That's it. Mass is a self-perpetuating topology within the event graph.

## Chapter 7: Pending vs Committed

---

If it's all events and operations underneath, how do we ever go from "now" to the "next now"?

In our model, the graph is always accumulating events. The graph is always growing.

Energy is always interacting, edges are always arriving. But those events don't produce the answer or the next now, until someone asks them. Our graph is lazy loaded.

This is the core mechanism of the quantum world in our model. Events accumulate, but they are not summed/committed until a downstream event _needs_ the result. We're going to call that downstream event a **consumer**.

No consumer? The events keep arriving. The node stays pending. Another event could still show up and change the output. This is like someone asking you a math question verbally.

You hear: "what does"…"3"…"divided by"…"3"…"_plus_"… "5"… "equal?"…

Until they say "equals?" you need to wait for all the numbers and operations to arrive. The graph works the same way. It keeps track of all the operations and values but until a consumer asks "equals?", it's waiting to answer. And this is important, the events arriving in sequence _change_ the output. Until the question is finished, the answer doesn't exist. Not because it's hidden. Because the computation isn't done.

So if these pending events are in our graph, and our graph is our reality, than they should show up in our reality.

And they do. Physicists call it a wave function. It's a probability distribution that describes where a particle _might_ be and what it _might_ do. When you measure the wave "collapses" and you get a single definite answer.

In our model, the wave function is what a pending node looks like from the surface. It's not that the particle is in many places at once. It's that the events haven't been summed yet. The answer genuinely doesn't exist. Not because it's hidden. Not because we're ignorant. Because the computation isn't finished.

### What Counts as a Consumer?

The truth is, I don't know exactly. What counts as an observer is an open question in physics. So I'm not going to pretend I've cracked it here. But for the sake of not being too hand-wavy, here's what I think it _ought_ to be.

Not every interaction in the graph can force a commit. Things are interacting constantly. Photons scatter off surfaces. Particles exchange energy. Momentum transfers. If every interaction forced a commit, nothing would ever stay pending and there would be no quantum mechanics. Everything would just be classical.

Energy exchange, scattering, momentum transfer — these should be operations, logged as edges. More edges adding terms to the pending computation. They contribute to the final output, but they don't demand an answer.

But there might be some clues as to what counts.

There's a famous result in quantum mechanics called the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. You can know a particle's position precisely, or its momentum precisely, but not both at the same time. The more precisely you pin down one, the more the other smears out.

Remember, in our model there is no particle. What we call a "particle" is a collection of interlinked events in the graph. Mass or things are a self-perpetuating topology. An electron isn't a single dot. It's a region of the graph with structure.

Here's the key idea: different measurable properties might correspond to different _subsets_ of events within that neighborhood.

Based on the Heisenberg uncertainty principle specific properties might have their own topological subsets within a given neighborhood. So you could read position while leaving momentum still pending.

Additionally when you read one property and force a commit, it produces outgoing edges — new operations that propagate forward. Those new edges don't just disappear. They flow into the rest of the neighborhood, including the momentum subset. They become additional operations added to the still-pending momentum events. They change the computation.

## Chapter 8: The Event Cone

---

Before we said events in our graph are the results of all the events that came before it.

But we have a strict speed limit in our graph (C), so not all events arrive at the same time.

In physics, your **light cone** is everything that could ever reach you, given the speed limit of C. Every event in the universe that is causally connected to you.

We're going to define something narrower. Your **event cone** is the set of events that _will_ reach a node while its still in its pending state, before its asked to commit.

For example, imagine your our brother is expecting a baby today. You're driving to a family function. You know, with absolute certainty, that someone at this function is going to ask: "are you an uncle yet?"

Right now, driving in, what's the answer?

It's not yes. It's not no, the baby could have been born. So... maybe? Your brother hasn't called yet. The order information flows to you matters. Does your aunt ask before your brother calls? Once your aunt asks, you are committed. Done.

If your brother calls you _before_ your aunt asks, than it is in that nodes event cone.

## Chapter 11: Delayed Choice

---

This experiment has another famous variation that we need to address: the delayed choice version.

In the standard setup, the detectors sit between the slits and the screen. But what if we move them? In the delayed choice version, we set up detectors that measure which way the photon went _after_ it has already passed through the slits. The photon is long gone — it went through, it's on its way to the screen — and only then do we look.

When we do this, the interference pattern disappears. Every time. It's as if the universe knew ahead of time that we would look, and the photon decided to behave like a particle the whole time. This is what makes physicists lose sleep.

Our model provides a different interpretation. Being in a pending state is the normal state of the universe. Events always start off as pending, then commit, then the next events accumulate, then commit. Pending isn't special. It's the default.

In our experiment, the photon is traveling as a wave and passes through both slits. An interference pattern is building — the operations from both paths are accumulating in the pending computation. Then the detector fires. It's a consumer. It asks "equals?" and forces a commit. The pending node resolves to a definite position.

But here's the key: the instant it commits, the next event is born. The photon goes right back to being a wave. It propagates from that single committed position toward the screen.

The interference pattern is destroyed — but not by magic, and not retroactively. It's destroyed because you forced a commit at a definite position, and the probability wave that emerged from that single location had nothing to interfere with. One source, one wave, no fringes. Over many photons, some commit at positions drawn from the interference distribution at the detector, but each one re-propagates alone. The ensemble of single-source waves blurs the fringes into a smooth pattern.

The universe didn't know you were going to look. It didn't need to. You just asked "equals?" before the screen did, and the answer you got became the only source for what came next.

To draw our experiment out to its logical conclusion: if you placed another pair of slits after the detector, the interference pattern should re-emerge. The photon is pending again. It would pass through both new slits, accumulate operations from both paths, and if no one asks "equals?" before the screen — fringes.

If this interoperation is correct, there would still be a interference pattern on where the position resolves for the detector. On extreme geometries and variations between the detector and screen this should resolve in some sort of noticeable difference between experiments that had no detection at all.

## Chapter 12: The Quantum Eraser (aka: The Final Boss)

---

Good lord this is getting fucking long. But the universe just won't chill bro.

There is one more variation of this experiment that we need to address. The Quantum Eraser.

The setup is more involved, so let's walk through it. Instead of sending a single photon through the slits, you create a pair of entangled photons. One — the signal photon — goes through the double slit and hits a screen. The other — the idler photon — gets sent on a detour through a series of beam splitters and mirrors to a set of detectors.

These detectors come in two flavors. Two of them (call them D3 and D4) preserve which-path information — if D3 clicks, you know the photon came from slit A. The other two (D1 and D2) combine the paths in a way that erases the which-path information. You can no longer tell which slit the photon came from.

Here's what makes people lose sleep: the signal photon hits the screen _before_ the idler photon reaches its detector. The choice to erase or preserve the which-path information happens _after_ the signal photon has already been recorded. And yet, when experimenters go back and sort the signal photon hits by which idler detector fired, the signal photons paired with D1 or D2 (the erasers) show an interference pattern. The ones paired with D3 or D4 (the which-path detectors) do not.

It looks like the future is reaching back and rewriting the past.

It isn't.

Here's what's actually happening, and it's less dramatic but more interesting. The total pattern of signal photons on the screen — _before_ any sorting — never shows interference. It's always the same smooth blob. Always. Regardless of what you do with the idler.

The interference pattern only appears when you _filter_ the data. You take the full set of signal photon positions and you separate them into subsets based on which idler detector clicked. One subset shows fringes. The complementary subset also shows fringes — but shifted by half a wavelength. Add them back together and they cancel perfectly, giving you the original smooth blob.

In our model, here is what's happening in the graph.

The signal and idler photons are entangled. In graph terms, their pending computations share edges — they are not independent nodes. They are linked regions of the graph whose outcomes are correlated.

The signal photon travels through both slits as a pending node and hits the screen. The screen is a consumer. It forces a commit and records a position. But because the signal photon's pending state is entangled with the idler — because its computation includes edges from a node that hasn't committed yet — the position it commits to is drawn from the _combined_ state, not from a clean two-slit wave function.

That combined state already encodes the entanglement. It already contains two sub-populations: one corresponding to idler-goes-to-D1/D2 and one corresponding to idler-goes-to-D3/D4. These sub-populations produce complementary interference patterns that, when summed, wash each other out. The commit at the screen samples from this total distribution, which has no fringes.

Nothing changes later. The idler photon eventually reaches its detector. That's another commit in the graph — but it doesn't alter the signal photon's already-committed position. What it does is give you a _label_. It tells you which sub-population each signal photon belonged to. When you use that label to sort, the hidden structure appears.

The reason this feels like backward causation is because people expect the signal photon's pattern to change based on a future choice. But the signal pattern never changes. It's the same blob every time. The "eraser" doesn't erase anything that happened at the screen. It gives you information that lets you _partition_ the data you already collected. The physics happened at the moment of entanglement — at the moment those two nodes became linked in the graph. Everything after that is just bookkeeping.

This is why the quantum eraser is not mysterious in our model. It's not time travel. It's not retrocausality. It's entanglement creating correlated pending computations in the graph, those computations committing at different times, and the correlations becoming visible only when you have both pieces of information.

The universe didn't rewrite anything. You just finished reading the ledger.
